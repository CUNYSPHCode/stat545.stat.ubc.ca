Learning Objectives

From today’s class, students are anticipated to be able to:

  • Become familiar with customizing ggplot2 graphs.
  • Choose an appropriate plot type for Exploratory Data Analysis.
  • Understand what makes data visualizations effective.
  • Understand how to customize and fine-tune ggplot2 graphics to create effective visualizations.

Resources

Video lecture for today:

  • ggplot2 Part 2: Plotting for Exploratory Data Analysis

Optional further reading:

  • Bite-sized resources to help you produce effective visualizations:
    • The “do’s and don’ts of effective graphics” in Jenny Bryan’s STAT 545 book provides some rules of thumb for producing effective visuals.
    • Vincenzo’s “Communicating data” slides provide some rules of thumb.
    • Callingbull.org’s entry on visualizations, by Carl T. Bergstrom and Jevin West, goes over several examples of improving ineffective visuals.
  • Books to help you produce effective visualizations:
    • Fundamentals of Data Visualization by Claus Wilke is an excellent guide to designing effective visuals.
    • Visualization Analysis and Design by Tamara Munzner is a gold-standard book for designing plots with a focus on human perception.
  • As before, the ggplot2 book by Hadley Wickham provides a great overview of ggplot2.

Demo 1

From last class:

Compute a weighted mean of life expectancy for different years, weighting by population.

Plot that global average life expectancy over time.

gapminder %>% 
  group_by(year) %>% 
  summarise(wmean = sum(lifeExp * pop) / sum(pop)) %>% 
  ggplot(aes(year, wmean)) +
  geom_line()
## `summarise()` ungrouping output (override with `.groups` argument)

Demo 2

From last class:

For each continent and year, how many countries have a “low” life expectancy? Let’s say life expectancy is “low” if it’s less than the median most recent life expectancy for each continent.

Plot the number of “low life expectancy” countries over time for each continent.

gapminder %>% 
  group_by(continent) %>% 
  mutate(median = median(lifeExp[year == 2007])) %>% 
  filter(lifeExp < median) %>% 
  count(continent, year) %>% 
  ggplot(aes(year, n)) +
  geom_line(aes(colour = continent)) +
  ylab("# of countries with low lifeExp") +
  labs(title = "Evolution over time") +
  scale_x_continuous("") +
  scale_colour_discrete("") +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(panel.grid = element_blank())
